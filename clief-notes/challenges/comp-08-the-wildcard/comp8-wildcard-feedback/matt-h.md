The shape is right: a founder's qualification judgment externalized into an ordered, citable rubric, so every tier is contestable line by line against a one-page contract. The FLAG outcome — hold the tier, ask exactly one question, default safe — is a genuinely good fifth answer where most systems either guess or stall, and the 2b-lite distinction (evaluating the category is not naming a broken system) is the kind of edge a real operator learns and a generic rubric misses.

The push is uncomfortable because it lands on your central promise. The pitch is a numeric score with cited rules; two of your four worked examples fail their own arithmetic — Example 1 cites five rules summing to ten and prints nine, Example 3 cites rules summing to five and prints six. Worked examples are teachers: right now they teach the drift. Fix both, add a show-the-addition line to the output contract, and collapse the three copies of your numbers (rules, rubric, icp) into one canonical source — your README currently tells a stranger to edit the copy the model doesn't score from. The audit-trail idea is sound; it just has to survive its own audit.
