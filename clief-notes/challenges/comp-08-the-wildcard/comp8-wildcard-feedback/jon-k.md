Sort by what a file is, not what it's about. That's a librarian's distinction — form over subject — and it's the single load-bearing rule that makes The Steward work where keyword sorters fail: a dated standup that mentions a project is a log, and your tie-breaker knows it ("It reads like active work — that's the trap"). But the thing that sets your repo apart is the proof folder. Locked predictions before each run, a cold test in a fresh session, an external stranger's run with the verbatim reply preserved, a control case that must not fire — and a gap-to-patch log where I could verify each patch actually landed in the shipped rules, including the miss you documented instead of hiding. Almost nobody in this field shipped evidence discipline like that; the one build that beat it won the comp. The propose-then-ratify taxonomy rule is the augmentation philosophy done properly: "a proposed area is not a real area until you accept it," because otherwise last run's guess silently becomes this run's structure.

Two pushes. Your system preaches accretion and its own runs evaporate — write each run's summary (files moved, stakes surfaced, areas proposed) to a dated log, which also gives you the undo manifest a mover of files needs; Joshua Hubbard's change log with one-step undo lines is the shape. And your LICENSE file is two bytes of nothing in a repo explicitly built for strangers — pick a license.
