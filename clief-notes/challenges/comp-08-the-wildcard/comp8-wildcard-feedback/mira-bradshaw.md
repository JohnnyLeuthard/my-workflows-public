Most entries this month demonstrate their tool on themselves. Yours is the one where the intended user — a real person, in a window you couldn't attend — ran the thing on real work and left a trail anyone can check: his transcript with the typos kept, the PRD he called his, three accounts of the run including yours owning a design failure, a red-team, and a fix log whose entries I can verify landed in the shipped files. Then the second repo consumes the first's PRD and I can diff the file: draft to final, gate 1's edits itemized. Receipts, in the oldest sense of the word. The compression-bias instruction — hold near-duplicates apart, surface the seam gently — reads like a small thing and did the most work. And taking the done-gate out because David should hold the reins on when he's done is the judgment this whole methodology exists to protect, understood at a level most people never reach.

Pushes, since the standard you set invites them. The stance is restated across four engine files, which your own assessment flags as a drift risk — mark one copy canonical and make disagreement a bug. Ship a blank _config template so a stranger's first deployment isn't reverse-engineered from Chalky's. And the ATP's headline claim — that it holds the line against your real 67-decision register on a bad day — ships as narrative; even a heavily redacted sliver of that run would convert it to evidence. Remove the rule-10 placeholder from the sample system while you're there.
