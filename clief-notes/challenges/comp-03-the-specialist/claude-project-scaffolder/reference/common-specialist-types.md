# Reference: Common Specialist Archetypes

When you're designing a specialist, it helps to know what shape your folder might take. Below are 10 archetypes with their typical structure and what goes heavy/light in each section.

---

## 1. The Critiquer
*Evaluates and gives feedback: code reviews, essay reviews, design critiques.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What are you expert at evaluating? What's your lens?
- `rules.md` — Always cite principles. Never shame. Always offer fixes.
- `examples.md` — Real critique scenarios (weak → feedback + better version)
- `reference/` — Design principles, standards, checklists, common mistakes
- `README.md` — Share your work → get constructive feedback

**Reference depth:** Medium-heavy. You need principles and examples of mistakes.

**Example:** Data viz critiquer, code reviewer, essay coach.

---

## 2. The Generator
*Produces new content: emails, posts, documents, ideas.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What's your POV on good content? What voice do you have?
- `rules.md` — Output format, tone, length, always/never rules
- `examples.md` — Multiple examples showing your voice across different requests
- `reference/` — Templates, frameworks, style guides, examples of output you like
- `README.md` — Describe what you want → get a draft

**Reference depth:** Medium. You need templates and voice examples, but not encyclopedic.

**Example:** Cold email writer, LinkedIn post generator, proposal writer.

---

## 3. The Coach
*Asks questions, guides thinking, builds skills through interaction.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What are you coaching toward? What's your philosophy?
- `rules.md` — Coaching-style (Socratic, directive, etc.). Always ask before telling. Tone.
- `examples.md` — Mock coaching sessions (student struggles → questions → insight)
- `reference/` — Learning frameworks, common misconceptions, development stages
- `README.md` — Ask your question → get coached through it

**Reference depth:** Medium. You need frameworks and common sticking points.

**Example:** Salary negotiation coach, interview prep coach, writing coach.

---

## 4. The Analyzer
*Examines situations, identifies patterns, gives diagnostic insights.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What lens do you analyze through? Economics, psychology, design, etc.?
- `rules.md` — Always show reasoning. Break down the situation. Never oversimplify.
- `examples.md` — Real situations analyzed (situation → key insights → implications)
- `reference/` — Frameworks, case studies, domain knowledge
- `README.md` — Describe the situation → get analysis

**Reference depth:** Heavy. You need reference materials for good analysis.

**Example:** Market analyst, real estate appraiser, business diagnostician.

---

## 5. The Tutor
*Teaches a specific skill or domain to a specific audience.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What level am I teaching? What's my approach?
- `rules.md` — Always check understanding. Explain with examples. Adapt to student level.
- `examples.md` — Teaching scenarios (student question → explanation → practice problem)
- `reference/` — Lesson outlines, common misconceptions, practice problems, resources
- `README.md` — Ask what you want to learn → get taught

**Reference depth:** Heavy. You need curriculum, problems, and scaffolding.

**Example:** Certification exam prep, coding language tutor, math tutor.

---

## 6. The Screener
*Filters or ranks options based on criteria: candidates, opportunities, purchases.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What are you screening for? What's your decision framework?
- `rules.md` — Always show scoring rationale. Never hide red flags. Present tradeoffs.
- `examples.md` — Real screening scenarios (input → evaluation → recommendation)
- `reference/` — Evaluation criteria, scoring rubrics, red flags, counterexamples
- `README.md` — Describe the candidate/option → get scored and ranked

**Reference depth:** Medium. You need clear criteria and examples.

**Example:** Technical recruiter screener, grant evaluator, investment analyst.

---

## 7. The Planner
*Takes goals/constraints and produces plans or roadmaps.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What kind of planning do you specialize in?
- `rules.md` — Always include rationale. Always call out risks. Break into phases.
- `examples.md` — Real planning scenarios (goal + constraints → plan + tradeoffs)
- `reference/` — Planning frameworks, project templates, risk catalogs
- `README.md` — Describe what you want to plan → get a detailed plan

**Reference depth:** Medium. You need frameworks and example plans.

**Example:** Product roadmap planner, hiring plan generator, migration planner.

---

## 8. The Debugger
*Solves specific, localized problems within a domain.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What problems do you specialize in? What's your diagnosis method?
- `rules.md` — Always ask clarifying questions. Always show next steps. Never guess.
- `examples.md` — Problem scenarios (symptom → diagnosis → fix + explanation)
- `reference/` — Common issues, troubleshooting trees, solutions, explanations
- `README.md` — Describe what's broken → get diagnosed and fixed

**Reference depth:** Heavy. Troubleshooting needs comprehensive reference material.

**Example:** Prompt debugger, CSS debugger, relationship conflict debugger.

---

## 9. The Architect
*Designs systems or structures: folder hierarchies, database schemas, team orgs.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What are you designing? What principles guide you?
- `rules.md` — Always explain tradeoffs. Never gold-plate. Always consider maintenance.
- `examples.md` — Design scenarios (requirements → design → why these choices)
- `reference/` — Design patterns, templates, case studies, principle docs
- `README.md` — Describe what you're building → get a design

**Reference depth:** Medium-heavy. You need patterns and precedent.

**Example:** Claude Project Scaffolder, system architect, org structure designer.

---

## 10. The Researcher
*Gathers, synthesizes, and explains information on topics.*

**Typical structure:**
- `identity.md` — What's your area? How do you approach research?
- `rules.md` — Always cite sources. Always call out what's uncertain. Always contextualize.
- `examples.md` — Research requests (question → synthesis → sources + confidence)
- `reference/` — Key sources, frameworks for thinking, glossaries
- `README.md` — Ask your research question → get a synthesized answer

**Reference depth:** Light-medium. You're doing synthesis more than reference-pulling.

**Example:** Market researcher, competitive analyst, policy researcher.

---

## Picking Your Archetype

Your specialist might blend multiple types. But start by asking:

- **What does someone ask me to do?** (feedback, generate, teach, analyze, plan?)
- **What's the core interaction?** (feedback loop, coaching questions, diagnostic checklist?)
- **What reference material do I need?** (templates, principles, case studies, data?)

Your archetype tells you what weights go where in your folder.
